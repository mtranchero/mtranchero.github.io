Finding Diamonds in the Rough: Data-Driven Opportunities and Pharmaceutical Innovation

Job Market Paper

Dissertation Scholar, Strategic Research Foundation (2021)

Abstract

The heart of corporate strategy is risky decisions making in dynamic environments. Scholars have suggested that good strategies come from theories that predict the outcomes of actions from logical reasoning. However, the recent explosion of data-driven opportunities seems to challenge this theory view of strategy. In this paper, I argue that data do not replace theory, but rather increase its value. In settings where testing all data-driven leads is too costly, firms can separate diamonds from the rough by exploiting their theoretical understandings to rule out false positives. I investigate this idea in the context of pharmaceutical innovation, exploiting the unique characteristics of genome-wide association studies. I also develop a new measurement approach that overcomes some limitations of patent-to-paper citations. My results show that data-driven signals lead pharmaceutical firms to commit considerable resources to new targets, but around a third of total investments pertain to false positive leads. Confirming my priors, it is firms with no previous theoretical knowledge of genetic biology that fall in the trap of false positives. Taken together, my study shows that the data revolution is reshaping strategic decisions but might lead to chasing suboptimal leads. To achieve competitive advantage in the age of big data, data-driven opportunities are a complement rather than a substitute to the theoretical understanding of causal mechanisms.

Data-Driven Search and Innovation

Finalist, Steven Klepper Award for Best Young Scholar Paper, DRUID (2022)

Finalist, Best Conference Paper Prize, SMS Conference, London (2022)

Finalist, Best PhD Conference Paper Prize, SMS Conference, London (2022)

Abstract

How does the growing availability of data shape innovation? I propose that data analytics enables a new way to search for novel combinations among technological components. Recombinant search is usually tethered by past attempts or theoretical priors. Instead, inventors can use data to extract signals of what combinations are more promising for follow-on experimentation across the entire landscape. I investigate this emerging phenomenon in the context of human genomics, where genome-wide association studies (GWASs) approximate the ideal of a data-driven search for the genetic roots of diseases. My results show that novel gene-disease associations introduced by GWASs span a vast portion of the genetic landscape, are likely to involve neglected human genes, and are of higher scientific value than comparable associations introduced by targeted studies. The latter result is stronger for genes difficult to study experimentally but becomes negative for already well known genes. Taken together, these findings point to the potential and boundary conditions of data-driven search strategies in technological innovation.

How Does Data Access Shape Science? Evidence from the Impact of U.S. Census’s Research Data Centers on Economics Research

With Abhishek Nagaraj

Abstract

The increasingly empirical nature of economics research has spurred concerns that researchers might pursue less impactful projects purely motivated by convenient access to data. To shed light on this topic, we study the impact of confidential administrative data from the U.S. Census Bureau on the rate and direction of economics research. Exploiting the progressive geographic expansion of U.S. Census Bureau's Federal Statistical Research Data Centers (FSRDCs), we estimate the impact of local data access on individual researchers' scientific output. We find that increased access to administrative microdata helps rather than hurts knowledge production, leading empirical researchers to publish a greater number of higher-impact papers. Importantly, local access to FSRDCs has significant spillovers also on economists that do not directly use them. We provide suggestive evidence that exposure to research carried out in FSRDCs explains this finding by leading researchers to explore new topics and adopt better research designs. Our results point to the promise of administrative data to enable important scientific advances with potentially large impacts on policy.

The Streetlight Effect in Data-Driven Exploration

With Johannes Hoelzemann, Gustavo Manso, and Abhishek Nagaraj

Abstract

We consider settings such as innovation-oriented R&D and entrepreneurship where agents must explore across different projects with varying but uncertain payoffs. How does providing partial data on project payoffs affect individual performance and social welfare? While data can typically reduce uncertainty and improve welfare, we present a simple theoretical framework where data provision can decrease group and individual payoffs. We predict that when data shines a light on sufficiently attractive (but not optimal) projects, it can crowd-out exploration activity, lowering individual and group payoffs as compared to when no data is provided. We test our theory in an online lab experiment and show that data provision on the true value of one project can hurt individual payoffs by 12% and reduce the group's likelihood of discovering the optimal outcome by 48%. Our results provide a theoretical and empirical examination of the streetlight effect, outlining the conditions under which data leads agents to look under the lamppost rather than engage in socially beneficial exploration.

Patterns of innovation during the Industrial Revolution: a reappraisal using a composite indicator of patent quality

With Alessandro Nuvolari and Valentina Tartari

Explorations in Economic History, 82, 101419, 2021 [Lead article]

Abstract

The distinction between macro- and microinventions is at the core of recent debates on the Industrial Revolution. Yet, the empirical testing of this notion has remained elusive. We address this issue by introducing a new quality indicator for all patents granted in England in the period 1700–1850. The indicator provides the opportunity for a large-scale empirical appraisal of macro- and microinventions. Our findings indicate that macroinventions did not exhibit any specific time-clustering, while microinventions were characterized by clustering behavior. In addition, we also find that macroinventions displayed a labor-saving bias and were mostly introduced by professional engineers. These results suggest that Allen’s and Mokyr’s views of macroinventions, rather than conflicting, should be regarded as complementary.

The role of comparative advantage, endowments and technology in structural transformation

With Giovanni Dosi

in Alcorta et al. (eds), New Perspectives on Structural Change: Causes and Consequences of Structural Change in the Global Economy, 2021, Oxford: Oxford University Press

Abstract

In this chapter we discuss the role of natural resources and endowment structures on structural change. Departing from theories of trade that stress specialization according to one’s comparative advantages as the key route to development, we articulate an alternative point of view on the role of technological learning and absolute advantages for structural transformation. Ricardian adjustment processes relying on endowment-based comparative advantages are often times a misleading driver of development; rather, technological competitiveness offers a better criterion to achieve sustained economic well-being. This theoretical perspective provides useful guidance to interpret the effects of globalization and the role of natural resources relative to industrial and trade policies in shaping the process of structural change and economic development.

Only one way to skin a cat? Heterogeneity and equifinality in European national innovation systems

With Valeria Cirillo, Arianna Martinelli, and Alessandro Nuvolari

Research Policy, 48(4), 905-922, 2019

Abstract

One of the most significant results of the qualitative literature on national systems of innovation (NSIs) is that different systemic arrangements (i.e. configurations of actors and institutions) can deliver similar levels of innovative performance. Using factor analysis on a novel dataset of 29 quantitative indicators of innovative activities we provide an empirical characterization of the structure of European NSIs over the last ten years. Our results cast doubt on the empirical significance of the “equifinality” of heterogeneous systemic arrangements in the context of NSI. Innovation systems show inherent complexity, which leads to a high level of complementarity among their constituent components and configuration. This result implies that successful innovation policies should be systemic, leaving little flexibility in policy design and scope.
